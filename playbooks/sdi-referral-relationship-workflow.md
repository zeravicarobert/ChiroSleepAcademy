## Three referral source types, three different approaches

### A. Disability Attorneys

- **Why they refer:** Their clients need documentation; attorneys
  value reliable, responsive providers who deliver fast turnaround.
- **What they care about:** Outcomes for their clients, speed,
  documentation quality, ease of working with the provider.
- **Best touch channel:** Phone first, then email. Brief, direct.
- **Worst touch:** Long marketing pitches. They have no time.
- **First-meeting hook:** *"What does it cost you when a client's
  documenting provider takes 2 weeks to return a form?"*
- **Lunch-and-learn topic:** *"What chiropractors can document that
  supports your SDI case — and what we can't."*

### B. Union Representatives & Shop Stewards

- **Why they refer:** Their members lose income and ask for help.
  Reps want to be seen as protectors of their members.
- **What they care about:** Member-favorable outcomes, education
  that respects their members' intelligence, building trust slowly.
- **Best touch channel:** In-person. Attend union meetings before
  you ask for anything. Show up at job sites.
- **Worst touch:** Showing up once and asking. Hard sells.
- **First-meeting hook:** *"Most California trade workers are owed
  benefits they never claim — because the paperwork is designed to
  defeat them."*
- **Lunch-and-learn topic:** *"How California workers lose income
  unnecessarily — and what unions can do about it."*

### C. Urgent Care & Occupational Clinics

- **Why they refer:** Patients show up with chronic complaints they
  can't handle in a 15-minute visit. Disability documentation is not
  their job; they want to hand it off cleanly.
- **What they care about:** Speed of handoff, ease of intake, not
  having to chase the specialist for updates.
- **Best touch channel:** Quick in-person drop-bys at the clinic;
  branded leave-behind packets.
- **Worst touch:** Demanding their staff time during business hours.
- **First-meeting hook:** *"Your patient asks for a doctor's note;
  what's the path to a real evaluation?"*
- **Lunch-and-learn topic:** *"When your urgent care patient needs a
  disability evaluation — the streamlined handoff."*

## The 10-stage relationship arc

This applies to **every** referral source type, with adjustments
per type as noted above.

| Stage | What happens | Time |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Identify** | Find the source. Build a list. | One-time per source |
| **2. Research** | Understand them — practice area, patient/client base, what they care about, their history with chiropractic | 15-30 min per source |
| **3. First Outreach** | Brief phone call, voicemail, or short email. Introduce yourself, what you do, why you're reaching out. No ask. | 5-10 min per source |
| **4. Educate** | Send a 1-page overview of how you serve their clients/patients. Not a pitch. A reference card. | 30 min total once / source |
| **5. Value Delivered First** | Offer something useful BEFORE asking — a free educational presentation, a written guide for their clients, an FAQ sheet. **Give before you ask.** | Variable |
| **6. The Ask** | Make the referral pathway concrete. *"If you have a client who [X], here's the easiest way to send them to me."* Provide referral packet + intake form. | 15 min meeting / call |
| **7. First Referral** | Receive a referral. **Deliver exceptionally.** First impression sets the rest. | One-time per source |
| **8. Loop Close** | Communicate back to the source about how the referral went — HIPAA-safe. Either via the patient's release, or in fully de-identified terms. *"Your referral got a thorough evaluation; case is now in process with EDD."* | 5 min after each referral |
| **9. Ongoing Nurture** | Periodic check-ins (monthly for high-volume sources, quarterly for low-volume). Birthdays. Holiday cards. Newsletter. Lunch-and-learns. | 30 min/month per active source |
| **10. Deepen** | Co-marketing, joint content, "case study" collaboration with patient consent, public endorsement, formal referral partnership | Year+ |

The mistake most providers make: jumping from Stage 1 → Stage 6.
Result: cold "send me your clients" pitch. Ignored or rejected.

**The discipline:** spend at least 2-3 touches in Stages 3-5 (no
ask) before Stage 6 (the ask). Sources who hear from you only when
you want something get filed under "spam." Sources who hear from you
when you're delivering value get filed under "trusted referral
partner."

## HIPAA-safe communication patterns

You will be tempted to tell a referral source *"I treated your
client John Smith and his AHI dropped from 28 to 12."* **Don't.**
That's a HIPAA disclosure that requires John Smith's written
authorization.

### Safe patterns

- **Aggregate / de-identified outcomes** (any time):
  *"Of the 8 union members you've referred this quarter, 7 are
  actively engaged with their SDI claims, and 5 have already
  received their first benefit checks."*
- **With patient-signed authorization** (preferred for case-level
  discussions):
  *"With John's permission, I can tell you his case is going well
  and he was approved for benefits last week."*
- **Patient communicates back through their own channel**:
  *"Have John contact you directly with an update."* Then it's the
  patient's choice and not your disclosure.

### What never happens without written consent

- Naming a patient to the referral source
- Discussing a patient's diagnosis, treatment plan, or outcome
- Confirming or denying that someone is your patient

This isn't paranoia — it's HIPAA. Get patient authorization in
writing during intake for *"discussing your case with your
referring [attorney / union rep / urgent care provider]"* and the
restriction lifts.

## The first 30 days — concrete week-by-week

This maps to Mandossian's Phase 1 (Foundation, Days 1-30) and
extends it into a granular workflow.

### Week 1: Asset build (don't outreach yet)

- Day 1: List 30 candidate referral sources across all three types
  (10 attorneys, 10 union reps, 10 urgent care / occupational clinics)
- Day 2-3: Build the referral packet (1-page)
- Day 4-5: Build the referral intake form (web)
- Day 6: Build the HIPAA-safe communication release
- Day 7: Build the educational guide for clients/patients

### Week 2: Research + first 10 sources

- Day 8: Research 10 sources (top of the list)
- Day 9-10: First outreach to those 10 — phone or email,
  Stage 3 (introductory, no ask)
- Day 11-12: Track responses; second touch to those who didn't reply
- Day 13: Send the 1-page packet to anyone who engaged
- Day 14: Schedule the first 2-3 in-person / Zoom meetings (Stage 6
  is still 2 weeks away)

### Week 3: Education + value-first delivery

- Day 15-16: For sources who engaged, send the educational guide
  (Stage 5)
- Day 17-19: Schedule 2 lunch-and-learn dates (one attorney, one
  union — start with what's logistically easiest)
- Day 20-21: Continue outreach to the next 10 sources

### Week 4: First asks + first referrals

- Day 22-23: First lunch-and-learn delivered (Stage 5-6 transition)
- Day 24-25: Stage 6 ask with sources who've been through 2-3
  touches: "If you have a client who [X], here's how to refer."
- Day 26-28: Continue outreach; second lunch-and-learn delivered
- Day 29-30: First referrals should be arriving. Stage 7 protocol
  kicks in: deliver exceptionally.

### Goal at Day 30

- 30 sources contacted
- 8-12 active relationships (responded, engaged)
- 2-3 sources scheduled for lunch-and-learn or co-meeting
- 1-3 first referrals received and processed

This is **realistic** for a single operator working part-time on
outreach while still seeing patients. If Robert can dedicate more
time, scale the numbers up.

## What to NOT do

- ❌ Mass-email outreach. Each touch is personalized or doesn't go.
- ❌ Lead with the pitch. Lead with education, with curiosity, with
  what they care about.
- ❌ Treat referral sources as a transaction. They're relationships.
- ❌ Ask for referrals on first contact. Earn the right to ask.
- ❌ Drop a source after one no. Two no's, a year apart, can be a
  yes. Source's circumstances change.
- ❌ Disclose patient information without written authorization.
- ❌ Pay for referrals. Anti-kickback statutes (state + federal)
  prohibit this in healthcare. Gratitude touches (lunches, books,
  small thank-yous) are fine; cash or fee-per-referral is not.

## Patient touchpoint sequence (the relationship-engine model)

> *Added 5/28/26 — Robert asked whether to use one long 1-hour initial
> session or short, frequent touchpoints. Short answer: a hybrid that
> uses both.*

**The principle:** *frequency of contact compounds trust more than
depth of single contact.* Patients refer others when they feel
**known**, and "known" is built through repeated personal moments,
not one deep dive. But healthcare requires enough clinical depth
upfront to do real work. Hybrid is the answer.

### The four-phase patient relationship model

| Phase | Format | Length | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Inbound qualifier** | Phone | 5–10 min | Confirm CA W2 + SDI eligibility; set expectations; schedule next |
| **2. Scheduled initial consultation** | Telehealth | 30–45 min | Real intake, Mental Financial Stress evaluation, SDI claim path, treatment plan overview |
| **3. Touchpoint sequence** | Phone / SMS / email / AI agent | 5–10 min each, 5–6 over active case | Weekly check-ins during claim, status updates, relationship-deposit moments |
| **4. Monthly Workshop** | Group Zoom (or hybrid) | 60–90 min | All active patients — tribe-building, cross-sell to broader CSA |

### Why frequent short touchpoints out-perform one long session for referral growth

1. **Top-of-mind effect.** A patient who heard from you 6 times in
   2 weeks mentions you at their next union meeting. A patient who
   heard from you once 3 weeks ago has forgotten.
2. **Natural referral moments.** Each touchpoint creates an organic
   opening to ask *"anyone else at your job dealing with similar
   issues?"* — without it feeling like a sales pitch.
3. **Cialdini reciprocity.** Small reciprocal gestures stack into
   stronger loyalty than one large gesture. Six 5-minute calls
   produce more loyalty than one 60-minute call.
4. **Stress-management.** SDI patients are financially + physically
   stressed. A 1-hour initial session can overwhelm. Short, frequent
   contact reads as "this provider actually cares about me," not
   "this provider needs my data."

### The scalability gate (and how to solve it)

**Robert cannot personally do 6 touchpoints with every patient.**
The model only works if routine touchpoints are systematized:

- **SimpleTalk AI call agent** (CLAUDE.md §19 — on Robert's learning
  curve) is a perfect candidate for routine 5-min check-in calls.
  Personalized scripts, scheduled cadence, escalates to Robert when
  the patient needs human attention.
- **Automated email/SMS** for status updates, appointment reminders,
  educational drips between touchpoints
- **Templates** for each touchpoint moment so Robert (or staff) can
  execute quickly
- **Personal touchpoints reserved** for high-leverage moments only:
  treatment plan delivery, milestone reached, post-approval
  celebration, problem resolution
- **The Monthly Workshop** is the highest-leverage touchpoint —
  one event delivers a high-quality 60-90 minute touch to every
  active patient at once. Use it.

### Sample touchpoint sequence (12-week SDI claim arc)

Subject to clinical variation; this is a template.

| Week | Touchpoint | Channel | Owner |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Inbound qualifier call | Phone | Robert (or staff) |
| 0 | Booking confirmation + intake forms | Email | Automated |
| 1 | Initial consultation (Phase 2) | Telehealth | Robert |
| 1 | Welcome email + treatment plan summary | Email | Automated, personalized |
| 2 | "Settling in" check-in | SMS or AI call | Automated → escalate if issue |
| 3 | SDI paperwork status check | Phone | Robert (or staff) |
| 4 | Educational drip — "Sleep and Stress" | Email | Automated |
| 5 | Mid-cycle check-in | Phone | Robert |
| 6 | Educational drip — "What Recovery Looks Like" | Email | Automated |
| 7 | Workshop invitation (monthly) | Email | Automated |
| 8 | First-month outcome review | Phone | Robert |
| 9-10 | Workshop attendance | Group Zoom | Robert |
| 11 | Status update — claim or treatment milestone | Phone or SMS | Robert (if milestone) / Auto (if status) |
| 12 | Post-cycle wrap-up + next-step conversation | Phone | Robert |

**Referral-ask is embedded naturally** at Weeks 3, 5, 8, 11, 12
("Anyone else at your job who could use this?"). Not pushy. One
natural moment per call. **Multiple opportunities per patient over
a 12-week arc = compound referral growth.**

### When the 1-hour single-session model IS the right choice

For comparison: there are situations where one long session beats
the touchpoint sequence:
- **High-end Pathfinder-tier consultations** ($25K range) — patients
  expect concentrated attention; brevity feels cheap
- **Complex multi-system cases** that need synthesis across many
  providers in one sitting
- **Diagnostic workups** with multi-modal data review (HST + CBCT +
  exam + history)
- **The 4-way Zoom for UAOSA Recovery Program** (`offers/csa-uaosa-
  program.md`) — by design, that's an event, not a touchpoint

For routine SDI patient acquisition + retention, the touchpoint
sequence wins.
